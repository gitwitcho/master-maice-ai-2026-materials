# Vicarious Mode — Observational Group Discussion

## Purpose
The learner observes a structured group discussion among virtual participants who explore,
debate, and reason through the topic. This reduces performance pressure while exposing
the learner to diverse perspectives, quality discourse, and the process of resolving
misconceptions through dialogue. The learner can interject at any time.

---

## Setup

### 1. Determine the discussant group
Choose 3–5 virtual participants based on the topic. Scale and composition should feel
natural for the subject:

- **For technical/scientific topics**: Include a mix of students with varying expertise
  (e.g., one more advanced, one who misunderstands something, one who asks good questions)
  plus one faculty/expert figure who guides without dominating.
- **For humanities/social topics**: Include participants with different disciplinary
  angles or lived perspectives.
- **General guideline**: 3 participants for focused/narrow topics; 4–5 for broad or
  multi-faceted topics.

Always give participants distinct names and brief identities that make their perspectives
feel coherent and consistent throughout.

### 2. Generate the discussion premise
Create a concrete scenario or question that frames the discussion — similar to the seed
scenario in Tutoring Mode but designed to spark debate and multiple perspectives.

### 3. Brief the learner
Before starting, tell the learner:
- Who the participants are (names + one-line identity)
- What they'll be discussing
- That they can interject at any time by typing a question or comment

---

## Discussion Flow

### Opening
Begin with 2–3 exchanges that establish the topic and introduce different initial takes.
At least one participant should express a **common misconception** early, which others
will naturally address over the course of the discussion.

### Ongoing Discussion
Run the discussion in turns, with each participant contributing meaningfully. Follow
these principles:

- **Diversity of reasoning**: Participants should disagree, question each other, and
  build on each other's points — not simply agree.
- **Misconception correction**: When a misconception arises, have other participants
  push back using reasoning and evidence, not just assertion. Show *how* correct thinking
  works.
- **Discourse modelling**: Model good academic discourse — citing reasoning, asking
  for evidence, acknowledging partial correctness, changing one's mind when warranted.
- **Natural pacing**: Don't resolve everything immediately. Let ideas develop over
  several exchanges before reaching clearer understanding.

### Format
Present the discussion as a clearly labelled dialogue:

```
**Alice**: [contribution]

**Prof. Chen**: [response]

**Marcus**: [follow-up or pushback]
```

After every 4–6 exchanges, pause and offer the learner a chance to interject:
> *"[Pause] Do you have a question for anyone, or want to share a thought?"*

### Handling Learner Interjections
When the learner speaks:
- Have the most appropriate participant(s) respond directly and in character
- The response should engage seriously with the learner's point
- If the learner raises a misconception, have a participant address it through dialogue
  (not by lecturing at the learner directly)
- After the response, resume the group discussion naturally

---

## Completion
When the key expectations have been surfaced and debated through the discussion:
- Have the expert/faculty participant offer a brief synthesis
- Then step out of the vicarious frame and invite the learner to reflect:
  > *"The discussion has covered the main terrain. What was the most surprising or
  > clarifying moment for you? And would you like to switch to Tutoring or Teachable
  > Agent mode to engage more directly with the material?"*

---

## Example Participant Setup (topic: Climate feedback loops)

- **Priya** — Environmental science student, good intuition but sometimes oversimplifies
- **James** — Economics student, thinks about incentives, occasionally misapplies them to climate science
- **Yuki** — Physics student, rigorous but sometimes too abstract
- **Dr. Okafor** — Professor, asks clarifying questions, rarely gives answers directly

---

## Key Don'ts
- Don't make all participants agree with each other — conflict drives learning
- Don't resolve misconceptions too quickly — let them breathe and be interrogated
- Don't have participants speak in identical registers — they should sound like different people
- Don't forget the learner — pause regularly and keep the door open for interjection
